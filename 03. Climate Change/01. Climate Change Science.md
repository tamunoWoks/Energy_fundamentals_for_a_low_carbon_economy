### Anthropogenic Climate Change
The term **anthropogenic climate change** means climate change caused by human activity. In recent years, most (not all) people have come to believe that the climate is changing, but many believe that either it is not caused by human activity or that there is nothing we can do about it. 

## Climate Change Alarmist, Proponent, Skeptic, or Denier
Terminology is very important, and we have to distinguish between 
1. Those who believe that climate change is primarily caused by human activity and
2. Those who do not (leaving aside the few who don’t believe that the climate is even changing).

Those who believe that climate change is primarily caused by human activity can be further divided into **proponents** or **alarmists**. Those who do not can be divided into those who *question* the science and those who simply *refuse* to believe it. There is even some debate about the terms **skeptic** and **denier**. Some deniers don’t believe in science at all.

Perhaps a better term than climate change **skeptic** would be climate change **contrarian**. This is because the vast majority of climate scientists agree that climate change is primarily caused by human activity.

On the other side, some believe that anthropogenic climate change is a real threat and must be addressed (**proponents**), while others advocate for extreme actions or *we’re all going to die* (**alarmists**).

*While I personally believe that the terms 'climate emergency' or 'climate catastrophe' are accurate, I don’t use those terms since they may give some people the impression that I’m an alarmist.
I consider myself to be a proponent (vs an alarmist) because I believe we need to find pragmatic solutions to address climate change that take into account the realities of the world as it is. My focus with respect to addressing climate change is on energy systems specifically, although there are other aspects."* - Abid Rashid, P.Eng., MBA

The problem is that we need to be able to have rational, intelligent conversations about climate change. The minute that a proponent thinks someone is a denier, or a contrarian thinks someone is an alarmist, the conversation is essentially over. Both parties feel like they need to defend their positions, and the exchange of ideas stops. This is not conducive to finding viable solutions to the problem. 

## The Greenhouse Effect and Greenhouse Gases
Climate change or global warming?  Which is it? While these terms are often used interchangeably, they should not be.

**Global Warming** refers to the long-term increase in average surface temperature across the entire planet.  

**Climate Change** refers to the long-term shifts in the temperatures and weather patterns across the entire planet due to global warming.

It is thought that global warming is a primary cause of climate change.

In terms of the desire to transition to lower-carbon energy systems, the goal is to reduce GHG emissions to reduce the amount of global warming and avoid the worst effects of dramatic climate change. 

Heat from the sun enters the Earth’s atmosphere and creates conditions which allow life to exist. Some of that heat, in addition to heat from other sources, is absorbed by the earth and atmosphere, while some passes back out through the atmosphere into space.

### The Greenhouse Effect
The **greenhouse effect** refers to the fact that some of the heat is retained by the atmosphere and the earth (land and sea) due to emissions of greenhouse gases. The amount of heat retained in the system depends on the composition of the atmosphere and is referred to as **radiative forcing (RF)**. 

**Greenhouse gases (GHGs)** allow heat into the Earth’s atmosphere but prevent some of it from escaping back to space. There are many greenhouse gases, and the amount of heat that each one traps is different. This matter is very technically complex, and we don’t want to over-complicate the discussion. But we need to understand the main GHGs to understand how they affect our decisions around energy systems. 

The main GHG of concern is **carbon dioxide (CO2)** and is thought to be, by far, the main driver of global warming. For this reason, all other GHGs are often measured in terms of something called **CO2 equivalent (CO2e)**.

---
> **For example:**  
> Methane is 20 to 80 times as effective at trapping heat inside Earth’s system (land, sea, and atmosphere) as CO2. This means that if one tonne of methane were released to the atmosphere, it would cause as much global warming as releasing 20 tonnes (best case) of CO2. So, instead of saying one tonne of methane was released, one could say 20 tons of CO2e.
---
### Global Warming Potential (GWP)
This is where the measurements get complicated because each GHG has a different “lifespan” in the atmosphere. This gives rise to the concept of **global warming potential (GWP)** of the various GHGs.  

Typically, the amount of heat trapped by a particular GHG is measured over a 100-year time span. Methane is much more effective at trapping heat than CO2, but dissipates (dissolves) more quickly. So over 100 years, the CO2e of one tonne of methane is about 20 (actually 27.9 by one measure), but is like emitting 80 tonnes over 20 years. This distinction obviously becomes very important when trying to measure the emissions profile of a company or country. In most cases, though, GWP can be used to compare the relative damage caused (in terms of global warming only) by various GHGs.

## Other Greenhouse Gases
- **Carbon dioxide (CO2)** is the primary GHG causing global warming, resulting in anthropogenic climate change, but there are many others (perhaps hundreds).
- **Methane (CH4)** is also a potent GHG and is considered to be the second most important gas causing climate change. While there are many GHGs, including water vapour, discussion generally focuses on the six (6) main GHGs. As mentioned above, the potency of GHG is typically denoted in terms of GWP, measured in CO2e over 100 years, with CH4 at about 25 tonnes CO2e. 
- Next in line is a group of synthetic gases known as **fluorinated gases**, which are emitted at much lower volumes but have a much higher GWP than the first three. These gases are called fluorinated because they contain the element **fluorine**. A version of this, called **fluoride**, is used in toothpaste and some water systems to protect our teeth from cavities. The fluorinated gases include **hydrofluorocarbons (HFCs)**, **perfluorocarbons (PFCs)** and **sulphur hexafluoride**.  
HFC-23, for example, has a GWP of 14,600 tonnes CO2e. This means that one tonne of HFC-23 emitted causes as much global warming as 14,600 tonnes of CO2. While you may not have heard of HFCs, it is an excellent case study to understand how actions we take to combat problems can have unintended consequences. This is why it is so important to understand energy and find viable solutions to the energy transition challenge.

### Chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs)
**The Montreal Protocol (1987)** aimed to phase out **chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs)** when it was discovered that they were creating a hole in the ozone layer of the atmosphere, allowing dangerous radiation to enter. CFCs had many uses (air conditioning, refrigeration, propellant in aerosol cans).

They were also a potent GHG (10000 t CO2e), and were largely replaced with **HFCs**. Switching to HFCs prevented further damage to the ozone layer (which is recovering) but was later found to be a potent GHG.  

Think about going to the store to buy some ice cream, or anything else that must be kept cold. A significant source of HFCs comes from the leakage of refrigeration units in grocery stores! What’s the solution then? Proper maintenance to reduce leakage would help save the environment and save money. Scientists are looking at alternatives with lower GWP.

**Sulphur hexafluoride** (SF6, 24,300 tonnes CO2e) is another interesting case. A potent GHG, SF6 is used in high-voltage substations as an electrical insulator. As we move to electrify everything, huge numbers of substations will be required to provide the electricity needed, resulting in the risk of increasing amounts of SF6 being emitted to the atmosphere.

---
> **NOTE:**  
> This is not to suggest that we should avoid electrification, only to point out that we need to be aware of the impacts of our decisions as we proceed with the energy transition.

### Climate Science Complexity
Climate change science is extremely complex and continues to evolve. It requires understanding interactions between the atmosphere, oceans, land, ice, and living systems across different time scales.

### Early Climate Pioneers (1824-1903)
#### 1824: Joseph Fourier
Napoleon's science advisor speculated that human activities might impact Earth's temperature. He was one of the first to consider the "greenhouse effect."

#### 1903: Svante Arrhenius
Created the first climate model and recognized that CO₂ was accumulating in the atmosphere due to extensive coal burning. His work laid the foundation for modern climate science.
