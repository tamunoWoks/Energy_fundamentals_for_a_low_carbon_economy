# Transportation and Heating Energy Systems
Transportation and heating/cooling our spaces together account for the majority of global energy consumption—making them central to understanding how energy shapes our daily lives.

**Transportation** includes both personal travel and the commercial networks that move goods from production to consumption. These systems connect us to the world, but also connect us directly to energy costs we feel at the gas pump and on our monthly bills.

Our desire for comfortable indoor spaces drives significant energy use. We all want comfort, but understanding the financial and environmental costs empowers better decisions. Some impacts are visible—like urban smog—while others remain hidden, such as ecosystem disruption and extreme weather damage.

**Heating and cooling** are best examined separately since heating currently relies primarily on fossil fuels while cooling depends largely on electricity. It's also useful to distinguish between conditioning our homes and workplaces versus industrial heating and cooling, as these serve different purposes and operate at different scales.

## Oil and Gas Dependency in Transport Modes
Transportation accounts for 20% of Canada’s total energy use, second only to industrial use at 53%. This includes both passenger and freight transport. Different modes of transport include cars, trucks, motorcycles, buses, airplanes, trains, and ships. Each mode presents unique challenges in terms of reducing emissions and improving efficiency. Also, different types of fuels may be used for the various modes.

Transportation fuels are primarily derived from crude oil at refineries. Think about gasoline, diesel, jet fuel, and also heavy fuel oil (bunker fuel), often used in ships. Each fuel type has advantages and disadvantages. Recall our discussion about energy density.

Transport trucks use diesel fuel rather than gasoline since it has a slightly higher energy density. Energy density, however, is only one factor to consider. We also need to consider cost, availability, emissions, and convenience, among other things.

Bunker fuel is highly polluting but cheap because it's essentially the low-value residue left after refiners extract more profitable fuels. The shipping industry is under considerable pressure to modernize its fleet due to the pollution caused by using bunker fuel.

To address this problem, we must consider the cost of converting all the ships in the world to other energy systems. Then we have to decide which types of energy would be better in terms of pollution, cost, etc. Similar types of questions come up when we look at converting the energy systems of all modes of transport.  

This is an important discussion given the large proportion of energy use and therefore emissions attributed to the transportation sector. Each industry (shipping, aviation, freight, etc.) continues to look for solutions to address concerns about its energy consumption and pollution. The trucking industry is a good example.

---
> **Example:**  
> Diesel fuel is quite dirty, which is why many jurisdictions now mandate low sulphur diesel (LSD) to reduce sulphur dioxide emissions that cause smog and acid rain—both air quality and climate concerns. The next step was ultra-low sulphur diesel (USDL), which is now mandated in Canada and the US. It is unclear if recent political changes in the United States will roll back these mandates there.
---
The aviation industry is working on modifications to incorporate **sustainable aviation fuel (SAF)** to reduce emissions from flying. There have also been test flights using solar power to determine if this may be a viable solution. Obviously, any changes to the energy system used to power airplanes would have to be bulletproof since failures would be catastrophic. The most relevant mode of transport for most people, however, is passenger vehicles.

**Electric vehicles (EVs)** offer valuable insights into the challenges of transitioning energy systems across all transportation modes. Electric vehicles are now widely available, but the adoption of this technology has not been as rapid as hoped. The cost of these vehicles is prohibitively high, requiring governments to provide subsidies to incentivize people to switch from **internal combustion engines (ICEs)**.

British Columbia and Quebec had the highest uptake of EVs due to generous provincial government incentives, but recent reductions in government incentives have slowed the purchase of EVs considerably.

## Heating and Cooling System Types
Heating and cooling systems consume nearly half of the world's final energy—a staggering figure that underscores their critical importance in our daily lives. About 95% of this heating demand comes from three key sectors:
- Residential buildings
- Commercial properties
- Industrial facilities

When we focus specifically on buildings, heating and cooling solutions fall into two main categories. 

Small-scale systems serve residential homes, while large-scale **district heating and cooling (DHC)** networks support commercial applications like schools, hospitals, office buildings, and shopping centres. Even when temperature control isn't needed, buildings still require air circulation through ventilation systems. Together, these technologies form what we call Heating, Ventilation, and Air Conditioning (HVAC) systems.

### The Canadian Context
In Canada's climate, home heating takes priority, and many houses feature furnaces without air conditioning components. However, this landscape is shifting. Rising temperatures driven by climate change are prompting more Canadians to install cooling systems, reflecting a global trend where improving living standards are expected to drive massive increases in air conditioning demand.

According to the International Energy Agency, cooling energy demand will surge by 45% by 2050 compared to 2016 levels. The energy sources powering these systems differ significantly: heating predominantly relies on fossil fuels, while air conditioning draws mainly on electricity.
