# Introduction to an Electrified Future
Picture your morning coffee brewed by electricity, your commute powered by a battery, and industrial machinery running on clean electrons instead of fossil fuels. This electrified future is already underway.

**Electrification** — replacing fossil fuel systems with electric alternatives — is advancing across most economic sectors as part of **decarbonization** efforts to address climate change. However, some systems will electrify more easily than others, and each transition carries important implications that require careful consideration.

This widespread electrification will significantly increase electricity demand, necessitating major upgrades to our power grids. Critical minerals are essential for both electrification devices and grid infrastructure improvements, creating substantial opportunities for Canada to supply these materials domestically and internationally. As renewables like wind and solar provide growing shares of our electricity supply, their variable output creates new challenges. Energy storage technologies will become increasingly important to manage this variability, opening additional business opportunities in the emerging clean energy economy.

## Electrification as a Decarbonization Strategy
**Decarbonization** refers to energy solutions that reduce or eliminate carbon dioxide (CO2) and other GHG emissions from human activities.

The goal of decarbonization is to **mitigate the effects of climate change**. Many human activities contribute to the accumulation of GHGs in the atmosphere, but the burning of fossil fuels is considered to be the chief contributor. As stated before, **electrification** refers to converting systems that have traditionally used fossil fuels to electric systems. These systems may involve industry, transportation, heating and cooling, as well as electricity generation itself.

---
> Electrification is widely considered to be the fastest way to decarbonize the world economy.
---
Electricity is widely available in many countries, but many other countries experience extreme shortages, with some regions within countries having no electricity at all.

### “How is that electricity created?”
Many countries rely primarily on coal to generate vast amounts of the electricity they consume.

---
> Example:  
> China and India generate roughly 60% of their electricity from coal. Both, however, are aggressively pursuing renewables to address severe air pollution concerns.
---
Another important aspect of electrification as a decarbonization strategy is the cost. This includes the cost of the electricity itself, but also the cost of converting existing systems to run on electricity.  

Consider the case of registered vehicles. In Canada, there are about 25 million vehicles. In the US, the number is closer to 300 million! The cost of converting 300 million ICE vehicles to electrics will be mind-boggling. In addition to the cost, it will take a considerable amount of time.

## Current Fossil Fuel Applications Suitable for Electrification or Hydrogen Conversion
Electricity is widely available in Canada, while hydrogen is not. If we consider decarbonization in the transportation sector, we see that other types of alternative vehicles preceded modern EVs.

Think about propane vehicles, buses running on natural gas, and electric trains. None of these technologies was widely adopted due to a variety of challenges. However, modern decarbonization efforts have become more viable due to technological advancements.

While electrification of vehicles is the main focus today, some are looking at the viability of using hydrogen as a low-carbon fuel. Some say that electric vehicles are more suitable for applications where gasoline is currently used, but that hydrogen makes more sense for applications where diesel fuel is currently used. This assumes, of course, that hydrogen can be produced in sufficient quantities and that it can be delivered to the places where it is needed. **A VERY BIG ASSUMPTION!**
