# Introduction to an Electrified Future
Picture your morning coffee brewed by electricity, your commute powered by a battery, and industrial machinery running on clean electrons instead of fossil fuels. This electrified future is already underway.

**Electrification** — replacing fossil fuel systems with electric alternatives — is advancing across most economic sectors as part of **decarbonization** efforts to address climate change. However, some systems will electrify more easily than others, and each transition carries important implications that require careful consideration.

This widespread electrification will significantly increase electricity demand, necessitating major upgrades to our power grids. Critical minerals are essential for both electrification devices and grid infrastructure improvements, creating substantial opportunities for Canada to supply these materials domestically and internationally. As renewables like wind and solar provide growing shares of our electricity supply, their variable output creates new challenges. Energy storage technologies will become increasingly important to manage this variability, opening additional business opportunities in the emerging clean energy economy.

## Electrification as a Decarbonization Strategy
**Decarbonization** refers to energy solutions that reduce or eliminate carbon dioxide (CO2) and other GHG emissions from human activities.

The goal of decarbonization is to **mitigate the effects of climate change**. Many human activities contribute to the accumulation of GHGs in the atmosphere, but the burning of fossil fuels is considered to be the chief contributor. As stated before, **electrification** refers to converting systems that have traditionally used fossil fuels to electric systems. These systems may involve industry, transportation, heating and cooling, as well as electricity generation itself.

---
> Electrification is widely considered to be the fastest way to decarbonize the world economy.
---
Electricity is widely available in many countries, but many other countries experience extreme shortages, with some regions within countries having no electricity at all.

### “How is that electricity created?”
Many countries rely primarily on coal to generate vast amounts of the electricity they consume.

---
> Example:  
> China and India generate roughly 60% of their electricity from coal. Both, however, are aggressively pursuing renewables to address severe air pollution concerns.
---
Another important aspect of electrification as a decarbonization strategy is the cost. This includes the cost of the electricity itself, but also the cost of converting existing systems to run on electricity.  

Consider the case of registered vehicles. In Canada, there are about 25 million vehicles. In the US, the number is closer to 300 million! The cost of converting 300 million ICE vehicles to electrics will be mind-boggling. In addition to the cost, it will take a considerable amount of time.

## Current Fossil Fuel Applications Suitable for Electrification or Hydrogen Conversion
Electricity is widely available in Canada, while hydrogen is not. If we consider decarbonization in the transportation sector, we see that other types of alternative vehicles preceded modern EVs.

Think about propane vehicles, buses running on natural gas, and electric trains. None of these technologies was widely adopted due to a variety of challenges. However, modern decarbonization efforts have become more viable due to technological advancements.

While electrification of vehicles is the main focus today, some are looking at the viability of using hydrogen as a low-carbon fuel. Some say that electric vehicles are more suitable for applications where gasoline is currently used, but that hydrogen makes more sense for applications where diesel fuel is currently used. This assumes, of course, that hydrogen can be produced in sufficient quantities and that it can be delivered to the places where it is needed. **A VERY BIG ASSUMPTION!**

Consider the case of large transport trucks, which currently use diesel fuel. Most people are unaware of the huge number of trucks used across the country to move everything from TVs to tomatoes. The cost of this transport has a direct effect on the cost of the goods. Does it make sense to electrify these transport trucks? Some prototypes have been made, but it seems unlikely that they will be widely adopted.

---
> **Challenge:**  
> Consider the challenge of charging trucks that travel 2,000 to 4,000 kilometres. If these vehicles had to stop every 500 kilometres for an 8-hour charge, the added expense would be enormous.
>   
> Even if the truck stop had fast chargers that could reduce the charging time to 2 hours, fast charging reduces the lifespan of batteries and would add additional costs for battery replacements. This would also mean that every truck stop would require the equivalent of an electrical substation to deliver the volume of electricity required by several trucks.
>   
> Hydrogen would address the refuelling time problem, but this would mean that hydrogen pipelines would have to be installed at the truck stops.
---
Other modes of transport present different challenges.
- **Airplanes**, for example, cannot stop to recharge.
- **Ships** present similar challenges.
- **Trains** require huge volumes of energy, but some now use electric power, although they burn diesel fuel to generate the electricity (diesel-electric).
- Conversely, a fleet of **delivery trucks** in a city may be able to have easily swappable batteries that can be changed quickly at the warehouse when the truck comes to pick up more parcels.

The challenges mentioned above are substantial, but there are others.

Electrification presents different challenges in other applications. **Heating** is another sector considered to be a prime target for decarbonization. Likewise, industrial applications, often heavily reliant on fossil fuels, can be electrified in some cases. We come back to the concept of energy density, though, understanding that this would require massive volumes of electricity.

## Electricity Grid Fundamentals and Capacity
Let’s look at the electricity grid and what it would take to supply the quantities of electricity required if we “electrify everything.”

Many people turn on their lights at home without any understanding of how the electricity comes to be there. We can thank the **electricity grid!**

We need a basic understanding of what the grid is and how it works. The grid, its functions, and its vulnerabilities are extremely complex, and only a very basic description will be included here.

First, how do we measure the volume or amount of electricity? The basic unit is the watt (W). Consider a 100-watt light bulb in your home. If you were to leave the light on for one hour, it would consume 100 watt-hours (Wh). If you had 10 bulbs on for an hour, they would consume 1000 Wh or 1 kWh.
- 1000 kW is a Megawatt (MW)
- 1000 MW is a Gigawatt (GW)
- 1000 GW is a Terawatt (TW)

The grid consists of three main components: **generation, transmission, and distribution.**

### GENERATION
Electricity **generation** may occur at a coal or natural gas-fired power plant, a nuclear facility, a hydroelectric dam, or a wind or solar farm. It’s important to keep in mind that each of these sources has vastly different GHG emissions in addition to other environmental impacts. Also, keep in mind the initial capital cost of a generating facility and the ongoing operating costs, which can change over time, sometimes dramatically and unexpectedly.
### TRANSMISSION
The transmission system consists of high-voltage power lines to take the electricity from where it is generated to where it will be consumed. The first step is to pass the electricity through a **transformer** to increase or “step up” the voltage before it enters the transmission lines. This is necessary to reduce the *line loss* or the amount of electricity lost due to **resistance** in the power lines. Once stepped up, the electricity can flow for long distances (up to 500km) with only minimal line loss.
### DISTRIBUTION
**Distribution** begins when the electricity is moved through a substation, which is another transformer used to “step down” the voltage to levels that are safer for people to handle. The distribution grid, consisting of lines, poles, transformers and switching and protection circuits, delivers the electricity to the final users. These may be residential homes, commercial buildings (offices, schools, shopping centres) or industrial users like factories.

**Decentralized energy systems**, also called **distributed energy resources (DERs)**, are much smaller grid systems where small amounts of energy are generated much closer to a much smaller number of end users.

--- 
Electricity demand fluctuates throughout the day, week and year. Grid capacity must be sufficient to reliably supply not only the minimum demand periods, referred to as the **base load**, but also the highest demand periods, referred to as the **peak load**.

Peak loads often occur during electricity **rush hours**, typically in the mornings and evenings on weekdays. This is because many people are at home using electricity, and businesses are using electricity as people arrive at or depart from work. In recent years, Alberta has experienced peak loads that have come close to the capacity of the grid.

### The Alberta Electric System Operator (AESO)
AESO is responsible for the safe and reliable operation of the grid. In the past, they have requested that Albertans reduce electricity use when the peak load gets too close to capacity. **“Brown-outs”** or **“load shedding”** may occur when the operator temporarily shuts off power to a portion of customers to avoid grid failure.

Grid failures can be caused by overloads, but also other factors such as equipment failure or even a tree falling on a power line. Failures can result in **“blackouts”** for customers until repairs can be completed.

---
> Wide-spread blackouts are rare, but they have occurred.
---
Even modern grids like those we have in North America require massive investments to ensure reliability. Many other countries are in much worse shape. We are barely able to meet current demand, never mind the massive demand increases that will result from electrification. As the cost of renewables comes down to meet or beat current grid prices **(grid parity)**, we find that **grid integration**, or the ability to accept non-traditional generation sources into the grid, is presenting some challenges.

Electrification presents other challenges in addition to issues with the grid being able to supply the amount of electricity that will be needed.

So-called **“smart grid” technologies** will be needed to provide the flexibility required on both the supply and demand sides. If supply can’t keep up with demand increases, dramatic price increases can be expected. Another weak link in the drive for electrification is the requirement for vast amounts of critical minerals.

## Critical Minerals and Supply Chains
**Critical minerals** are the different materials that will be required to electrify the world economy. This applies to upgrades to the grid itself, but also to products that will supply and use electricity in the future.

Think about batteries for energy storage from **variable renewable energy (VRE)**, computer chips, cell phones, windmills, solar panels, etc. Some of these materials, like copper and aluminum, are common, while others, like lithium and cobalt, are much less common. We need to consider not only the raw materials themselves, but also the supply chains that process the raw materials and turn them into the products we need.

Canada developed a *Critical Minerals Strategy* in 2022 to help develop value chains to supply the world with the critical minerals needed for electrification. It lists 30 minerals that Canada should focus on. The goal is to contribute to the entire value chain from exploration and extraction processing to advanced manufacturing and recycling. The secure supply of critical minerals is crucial to enable electrification. Many of these critical minerals present unique challenges that must be addressed.

Consider **Cobalt**, for example, over 50% of which is supplied by only one country. The Democratic Republic of Congo (DRC) is experiencing significant challenges related to the recovery of cobalt. Serious concerns about child labour and sexual violence have been associated with the numerous small mining operations there.

The metal is used extensively to produce lithium-ion batteries, which are used in many products from cell phones to EV’s. Batteries are another weak link in the drive for electrification.

## Energy Storage Integration Needs
One of the greatest challenges associated with electrification is the need to **store** electricity so it’s available when we need it.

**VREs** like wind and solar require some sort of storage for the electricity they generate. At times, the electricity can be fed directly into the grid for immediate use. However, sometimes that electricity may be generated when we don’t need it, or the grid is unable to accept it.

Batteries are used to supply the energy we need for our mobile devices, like cell phones and EVs. The currently available lithium-ion technology is unable to store large amounts of electricity and has other drawbacks. There have been enough cases of these batteries exploding that many companies will no longer store electric bikes, for example, inside. New battery technologies are being developed, including the **vanadium redox battery**, which can store grid-level amounts of energy.

The most common way to store large amounts of electricity from VREs used globally is a system referred to as **pumped hydro**.

This involves using wind or solar power to pump water from a low elevation to a reservoir at a higher elevation when the wind or solar installation is generating electricity that isn’t needed. When the electricity is needed, the water is released from the reservoir and flows through turbines to generate electricity in the same fashion as a hydro dam. These systems require suitable topography to allow for the reservoir and sufficient elevation change to make the process efficient.
