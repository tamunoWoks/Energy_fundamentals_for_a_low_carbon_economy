# Introduction to Energy Landscapes: Today and Tomorrow
Energy supply and demand projections are challenging to make due to the numerous unknowns that influence our predictions. One thing is clear, though: we need more energy! Population growth and improving living conditions around the world will only drive the demand for energy higher. Regional supply and use differences mean that optimal solutions to our energy challenges may not always be the same.

There is a range of options to supply our energy needs, and one concept that we should keep in mind is **energy density**. Some energy sources have been in use for many years or decades, but can be improved for better efficiency. Others are still emerging, and it is unclear how they may evolve and to what extent they’ll become a part of our energy mix. As we strive to improve our standard of living, increased energy demand will require us to improve **energy efficiency** and reduce our **energy intensity**.

## Global Energy Supply and Demand Trends
Global energy demand continues to increase. The world uses over 100 million barrels of oil every day. Oil, however, is only part of the picture. While the demand for oil increased by only 0.8% in 2024, according to the International Energy Agency (IEA), the rate of growth for all energy sources was 2.2%, as other energy sources are starting to be used to satisfy growing energy needs. Renewables are increasingly being used to supply energy needs for new technologies.

Global energy supply growth came mostly from renewables (38%), followed by natural gas (28%), which is often called a **bridging fuel** because it produces lower emissions than oil while renewable production scales up.

Coal, unfortunately, came in third (15%) due to its low cost and availability. China and India are expected to use vast amounts of coal in the years to come as they work to provide basic energy services to hundreds of millions of people. Together with other emerging and developing countries, they accounted for over 80% of the global energy demand growth in 2024.

The demand growth in many of these countries is driven, not only by the need to provide modern energy to those who don’t have it, but also by increases in manufacturing, as richer countries look to take advantage of cheaper labour. Also, manufacturing is often cheaper in some of these countries since environmental laws are less restrictive.

This can be viewed as **“outsourcing”** our pollution to other countries, which may already be suffering from environmental damage due to climate change and large populations. Some countries are looking at border taxes on cheap, foreign-made goods to discourage this practice.

**Biomass** supplies almost 6% of the energy humanity uses. Traditional uses involved burning of organic materials (wood, crop waste, cow dung, etc.) for cooking and heating. These sources are still widely used in developing countries and have profoundly negative impacts on the environment and human health. Modern techniques are being used to transform organic materials into **bioenergy** (wood pellets, biogas, biofuel). Bioenergy can be used for power, heat, transport, and industry. The IEA sees a substantial role for bioenergy in the energy mix of the future.

## Population growth and economic development impacts 
**Population growth** will result in a significant increase in demand for energy in the future. The current world population of 8.2 billion people is expected to grow to 8.6 billion by 2030 and to 9.7 billion by 2050.

In addition, the increased prosperity of many people in the developing world will also lead to increased demand for energy. Projections of how much energy will be needed are difficult due to the wide range of variables involved, but the trend is clear: we need more energy!

ExxonMobil has historically been quite good at these types of projections. They estimate that 15% more energy will be required by 2050. Electricity demand is expected to double as more industries look to electrification to reduce emissions. Transportation demand includes both personal travel and commercial demands, including trucking, shipping, rail, and aviation. It remains to be seen how fast these demand sources will be transformed using electricity or possibly hydrogen.

## Regional Energy Supply and Demand Variations
Most of Canada’s oil and gas comes from the western provinces. There are substantial differences when looking at oil and natural gas production by province.

Alberta is by far the largest producer of oil and gas, with wells located in most areas of the province.

British Columbia produces much more natural gas than oil, with most wells located in the NE part of the province. Saskatchewan produces more oil than natural gas, and most wells are located in the southern part of the province.

Some provinces generate over 90% of their electricity from dams.

Hydropower is prominent in Quebec, British Columbia, Manitoba, Yukon, and Newfoundland and Labrador. Much of this electricity is exported to the United States. 57% of Canada’s electricity comes from hydro, while 14% comes from nuclear facilities. Only about 7.5% comes from wind and solar, with coal (3.7%) and natural gas (15.2%) making up most of the remainder.

Energy demand is highest in Alberta, with Ontario and Quebec following behind. Industrial uses (factories, refineries, etc.) make up over 50% of energy use, with the transportation sector second at about 20%. Residential use accounts for almost 14%, while commercial uses (stores, businesses) are just behind at 13% (CER). For comparison, British Columbia uses almost one-third of the energy consumed by Alberta, while Saskatchewan and Manitoba combined use less than British Columbia!

## Energy Density Comparisons Across Fuel Types
**Energy density** refers to how much energy we can extract from a given amount of fuel. We can evaluate different fuels by comparing how much heat or electricity they produce.

For example, we can compare how much of different fuels we would need to heat our home for one year:
**Uranium** has a very high energy density, so a small cube would only be needed to provide enough energy to heat your home (if you had a nuclear reactor).
**Hydrogen** is also energy dense, but about 500 times less than uranium. This means you would need 500 times as much hydrogen to heat your home.
**Natural gas**, which is used extensively in some provinces, has about one-third the energy density of hydrogen, so you would need three times as much.

Other fuels that may be used, in decreasing order of energy density, include diesel fuel, heating oil, coal, and wood.

As the energy density of the fuel decreases, we need more and more of it to produce the same amount of heat. Of course, many other factors affect the desirability of any given fuel source. Think about emissions of greenhouse gases (GHGs) and other pollutants, cost, availability, safety, and convenience, among other things.

The concept of energy density does not consider these other factors, but it is still useful in terms of addressing the amount of energy humanity needs.
