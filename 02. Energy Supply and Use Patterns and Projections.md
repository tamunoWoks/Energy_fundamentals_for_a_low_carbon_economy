# Introduction to Energy Landscapes: Today and Tomorrow
Energy supply and demand projections are challenging to make due to the numerous unknowns that influence our predictions. One thing is clear, though: we need more energy! Population growth and improving living conditions around the world will only drive the demand for energy higher. Regional supply and use differences mean that optimal solutions to our energy challenges may not always be the same.

There is a range of options to supply our energy needs, and one concept that we should keep in mind is **energy density**. Some energy sources have been in use for many years or decades, but can be improved for better efficiency. Others are still emerging, and it is unclear how they may evolve and to what extent they’ll become a part of our energy mix. As we strive to improve our standard of living, increased energy demand will require us to improve **energy efficiency** and reduce our **energy intensity**.

## Global Energy Supply and Demand Trends
Global energy demand continues to increase. The world uses over 100 million barrels of oil every day. Oil, however, is only part of the picture. While the demand for oil increased by only 0.8% in 2024, according to the International Energy Agency (IEA), the rate of growth for all energy sources was 2.2%, as other energy sources are starting to be used to satisfy growing energy needs. Renewables are increasingly being used to supply energy needs for new technologies.

> **For example:**
> Enbridge recently announced a $900 million investment to support one of Meta’s data centres in Texas.

Global energy supply growth came mostly from renewables (38%), followed by natural gas (28%), which is often called a **bridging fuel** because it produces lower emissions than oil while renewable production scales up.

Coal, unfortunately, came in third (15%) due to its low cost and availability. China and India are expected to use vast amounts of coal in the years to come as they work to provide basic energy services to hundreds of millions of people. Together with other emerging and developing countries, they accounted for over 80% of the global energy demand growth in 2024.

The demand growth in many of these countries is driven, not only by the need to provide modern energy to those who don’t have it, but also by increases in manufacturing, as richer countries look to take advantage of cheaper labour. Also, manufacturing is often cheaper in some of these countries since environmental laws are less restrictive.

This can be viewed as **“outsourcing”** our pollution to other countries, which may already be suffering from environmental damage due to climate change and large populations. Some countries are looking at border taxes on cheap, foreign-made goods to discourage this practice.

**Biomass** supplies almost 6% of the energy humanity uses. Traditional uses involved burning of organic materials (wood, crop waste, cow dung, etc.) for cooking and heating. These sources are still widely used in developing countries and have profoundly negative impacts on the environment and human health. Modern techniques are being used to transform organic materials into **bioenergy** (wood pellets, biogas, biofuel). Bioenergy can be used for power, heat, transport, and industry. The IEA sees a substantial role for bioenergy in the energy mix of the future.
