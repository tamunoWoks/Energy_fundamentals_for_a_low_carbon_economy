# Introduction to Energy Literacy and Energy Transition
Energy ecosystems involve several key players. At times, the interests of these players may not align. This can lead to challenges in implementing the so-called **Energy Transition**, which means moving away from fossil fuel systems towards other energy sources. The transition also applies to how we use energy in every aspect of our lives.

## The Definition and Scope of Energy Literacy
Everything is all about energy. From the simplest element, hydrogen, with one electron spinning around one proton, to entire galaxies swirling around the universe, everything is all about energy.  
When we talk about energy literacy in this scope, it’s important to understand how we use energy, where it comes from, and how these things are changing as we move into the future.

### ENERGY LITERACY
Energy literacy means understanding the supply and demand dynamics of the worldwide energy landscape. It means understanding the factors that are, and will, affect this landscape. Those hoping to help solve the immense challenge that energy security presents for humanity may take a broad view, trying to understand the “big picture,” or may be very focused on one or more particular aspects of the energy landscape.  
When we think about energy security, we can imagine a world where all people have access to reliable, affordable, and responsible energy supplies.

### ENERGY INDEPENDENCE
Energy independence means that a country has enough energy to meet its own needs. Many countries, however, have enough energy to allow exports, while still requiring energy imports to meet their own needs. This includes Canada and the United States. In Canada, most of our oil and gas is produced in the western provinces. Due to transportation costs and lack of infrastructure (primarily pipelines), Eastern Canada imports crude oil from other countries to fuel their refineries.  
The picture becomes even more complicated when we consider refined products like gasoline, diesel, and jet fuel, among others.  

Fossil fuels supply about 80% of all energy used by humanity. This includes crude oil, natural gas, and coal. While there are huge variations in supply sources between countries and even within countries, it is important to keep in mind that it will be difficult to change this in the short term.  

--- 
> As we increase our level of energy literacy, our use of terminology becomes critical. We hear terms like clean energy, green energy, renewable energy, and alternative energy, but these terms often mean different things to different people. Unfortunately, no authority can define these concepts in absolute terms.
> 
---
### ALTERNATIVE ENERGY
Some may define all energy that is not fossil fuels as alternative energy. In this case, hydroelectric (dams) and nuclear energy would be defined as alternative energy. Others may argue that these energy sources have been widely used for decades and therefore should not be considered alternative sources.

### GREEN ENERGY
The term green energy is similarly difficult to define. Some would say that there is no such thing as green energy since all energy sources have some type of impact on the environment. Some have tried to address this problem by using the term **low-carbon energy**. This term would include nuclear energy, which has a much lower impact in terms of climate change, but has many other potentially adverse effects on humanity.

### RENEWABLE ENERGY
Renewable energy is also problematic because climate change may result in changes to the environment that could render some of these sources unreliable. For Example, consider dams, which rely on rivers flowing with sufficient volumes of water to replenish reservoirs to generate electricity. In 2024, many countries around the world suffered water shortages that prevented them from generating electricity from their dams. Wind patterns are also changing due to climate change and may result in reduced capacity of wind farms to provide electricity.

---
> Energy literacy requires us to understand these terminology challenges. It also requires us to understand, and sometimes clarify, how others are using these terms. Indeed, discussions about the terminology itself are a critical component of increasing our own energy literacy and helping others to increase theirs. Some individuals intentionally use ambiguous terms to mislead others or to make their point of view seem more convincing. We can be helpful by being prepared to ask clarifying questions and by being careful not to “go down rabbit holes,” which distract from the point of the conversation. Sometimes it's best to just agree to disagree!

In summary, energy literacy encompasses how we use energy and where it comes from.  
The energy transition refers to the desire to move away from fossil fuels toward clean (or is it green?) energy sources.  
As it turns out, there is no such thing as clean energy. ALL energy sources have impacts, but many people are unaware of what it takes to provide the energy that allows us to enjoy our modern way of life.  
Perhaps the term 'energy evolution' would be more appropriate, since it seems that humans will be using fossil fuels for the foreseeable future.

## Societal Benefits of Increased Energy Awareness
Despite being so central to our very way of life, many, if not most, people don’t think about the implications of where we get our energy from and how we use it.  
The choices about how we’ve used energy and where it came from have had profound impacts on humanity and the planet. Yet these decisions have largely been made behind the scenes with little discussion about the choices and the implications. **That must change!**   

Special interests can no longer make these decisions without input from the public. Societies must be made aware of energy sources and how they are used so that our input can be useful.  
Energy awareness allows people to guide the future by electing politicians who are committed to our priorities. Energy awareness also allows people to make individual choices that align with their priorities. Some people may be unaware of how their choices today may affect them in the future. Our choices affect our health, our economy, the environment, and the quality of life of people around the world. Individuals and societies will benefit from increased energy awareness.

## Energy Literacy as a Dynamic, Evolving Field
Energy literacy is a rapidly evolving field of study. Even traditional energy sources are transforming as new technologies emerge from the drive to improve performance.

### COAL
Coal use, for example, is being transformed with new technologies as engineers try to achieve the goal of “clean coal.” Perhaps “less dirty” coal is the short-term goal since many countries still burn coal due to its low cost and abundance. This is expected to continue for years to come.

### OIL AND GAS
The oil and gas industry is also evolving, both in terms of recovery and transportation. Horizontal drilling has been around for years, but the technology continues to improve, resulting in longer wells and reduced impacts. Hydraulic fracturing has been used for decades and continues to evolve to increase hydrocarbon recovery and conserve water resources.

### WIND AND SOLAR
Other energy sources are also becoming increasingly efficient. The cost of wind and solar power has been reduced dramatically in recent years, and is sometimes on par with oil and gas. The volume of energy they can provide, however, is still much less than fossil fuels and much less than what humanity requires. Our rapidly increasing demand for energy will require innovations.

---
> The cloud computing revolution is resulting in massive increases in demand for energy. Data centres contain huge numbers of computers and consume additional energy for every picture and video we save. Artificial intelligence (AI) is also adding to the computing power required and is expected to grow exponentially. These new types of energy consumption are in addition to the additional energy supply needed to bring billions of people to current Western living standards.

## Key Players in the Energy Ecosystem
We can think about energy ecosystems on four levels:

**1. Technologies**  
First, we need technologies that work.  

New technologies often take considerable time to optimize. As the bugs are worked out, new problems can arise. Trial and error often play an important role in the development of technologies. This is true when we consider new technologies, but also when we are modifying older technologies to address concerns that were previously unknown or considered unimportant.  

Technical expertise is an important part of this level in developing energy ecosystems. Engineers and technologists must be educated and trained to take advantage of the most recent developments in various scientific fields. This training takes many years. Further, it is crucial that a pipeline of young professionals be continuously cultivated. This becomes increasingly difficult as rapid technological advances become the norm.

**2. Businesses**  
We need businesses to scale technologies and bring them to societies effectively.  

The technology has to work, but then businesses have to be successful to bring any given technology into the market. Even proven technologies sometimes fail to be adopted because there is no business case to justify their widespread adoption. This means that another pipeline of young professionals is required, specifically in the field of business. These people must be educated in new business models that develop as economies evolve.

**3. Government Policies**  
We need government policies to enable the development of technologies and businesses.  

Government policies, rules, and regulations must be conducive to the development of technologies and businesses. Stability is a key to success and is difficult to achieve since governments change and often have opposing views on the best path forward. Even without a change of government, policies can change, resulting in substantial setbacks to technologies and businesses.

**4. People**  
Finally, People, all of us, must embrace new ideas and adopt new technologies for success.  

The public has a massive impact on these three other levels. We elect governments, educate our children, and make individual decisions that have long-lasting effects on the energy ecosystem. For example, the government decided to mandate that all new vehicles be electric by 2035. Car manufacturers made impactful business decisions to prepare for this change. The public, however, has not embraced electric vehicles (EVs) as expected, and it now seems unlikely that this target is realistic.
