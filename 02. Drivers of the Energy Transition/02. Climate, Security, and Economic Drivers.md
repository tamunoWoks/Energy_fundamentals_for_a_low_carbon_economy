# Introduction to What's really driving the energy transition?
The energy transition touches your life every day—from your utility bill to national security concerns. Now let's explore the key forces reshaping our energy landscape and why understanding them matters for your future.

Climate change is driving change, but it's not the only factor. Energy security and geopolitical tensions are forcing countries to rethink their energy strategies, especially after recent global events exposed vulnerabilities in our systems.

### The Economics of Energy
Think of energy choices like grocery shopping: you want quality, but you're watching costs. Just as you might pay more for organic food to avoid health risks, communities face similar trade-offs with energy sources.

**The risk factor:** Sometimes paying more upfront for reliable, cleaner energy saves money later by avoiding power outages, price spikes, or environmental damage. We must examine how market forces, price volatility, and risk planning are creating both opportunities and challenges in the energy transition, to understand what's happening in the energy world and why it affects your community and wallet.

## Climate Change as a Transition Driver
Climate change is the main driver of the energy transition. It is useful here to understand why it is considered to be the main driver of transition.

---
> "Many people do not believe that climate change is primarily caused by human activity, referred to as ‘anthropogenic climate change’. As an oil guy, I can assure you that it was not an easy place for me to go! However, at the end of the day, I’m a scientist (engineer) and the vast majority of experts believe it is and thus the need to ‘transition’ to low carbon fuel sources." - Abid Rashid
---
Climate change science is extremely complex, but it is widely understood that CO2 in the atmosphere is trapping heat on the Earth and causing average global temperatures to rise.  

---
> *CO2 comes from many sources, but the human-made variety primarily originates from the burning of fossil fuels.*
---
There are many other GHGs that come from human activity, but CO2 is the largest contributor to climate change, followed by methane (natural gas). Therefore, the fastest way to mitigate (reduce) the devastating effects of climate change is to reduce the amount of CO2 (and methane) that we’re putting into the atmosphere. This means reducing the amount of fossil fuels used in our energy systems and also reducing the amount of energy we use overall.

Politicians rarely discuss reducing energy consumption. It's unpopular to ask people to change their lifestyle, particularly for an issue like climate change that they may not see or understand in their daily lives.

*"Since energy costs money, we can save our own money by reducing our energy consumption. My friend told me sometimes he needs a blanket when watching TV since his air conditioning is set so low! He could save money and reduce climate change by setting the temperature a bit higher!" - Mr. Rashid*

Reducing energy consumption and switching to low-carbon fuels both play an important role in mitigating climate change. Climate change, however, is not the only thing driving the energy transition.
